One of the most honest questions we can ask before selling anything is: does this actually solve a problem for this organization?

The answer isn't always yes.

## Signals that it makes sense

There are certain patterns that appear in organizations where AI can generate real value:

**Information trapped in unsearchable formats**
If key knowledge lives in PDFs, emails, chats or in the heads of a few people, a knowledge system can help.

**Predictable processes that repeat constantly**
If your team goes through the same sequence of steps dozens of times per week, that process is a candidate for automation.

**High volume of repetitive inquiries**
If your clients or users ask the same things constantly, an agent can handle that without human intervention.

**Human bottlenecks in simple processes**
If there are steps in a process that only move forward when a specific person approves or responds — and that approval is predictable — there's value there.

## Signals that it doesn't make sense yet

There are also cases where AI isn't the right answer:

- The underlying problem is cultural or about leadership, not technology
- Processes are so chaotic that they need to be documented first
- Volume is so low that the cost isn't justified
- There isn't enough documentation to feed the system

In those cases, the most useful thing isn't to sell technology. It's to tell the organization it isn't ready and what it needs to fix first.

## The question that matters most

Before any technical evaluation, there's a simple question:

*What would your team do with the time or resources this system would free up?*

If the answer is vague or nonexistent, the project isn't justified. If the answer is specific and valuable, it's probably worth exploring.
